# Part F: Mini Security Consultant Report



---

## Executive Summary

Following a thorough review of the college's digital assets, infrastructure, and current security practices, this report identifies the **top five cybersecurity risks** currently facing the institution and provides **five targeted recommendations** to address them. Implementing these measures will significantly strengthen the college's security posture, protect student and staff data, ensure business continuity, and support compliance with data protection regulations.

---

## Top 5 Risks

### Risk 1: Unpatched & Outdated Systems
Many systems across the college are running outdated software that has not received recent security updates. This creates known, exploitable vulnerabilities. The WannaCry ransomware attack — which caused over $4 billion in global damage — succeeded primarily because organizations failed to apply a patch that had been available for two months. The college faces the same risk today.

**Likelihood:** High | **Impact:** Critical

---

### Risk 2: Phishing & Social Engineering Attacks
Staff and students regularly receive email communications, making them prime targets for phishing attacks. A single successful phishing email can expose login credentials, install malware, or give an attacker full access to college systems. Without structured, ongoing training, users remain the most vulnerable point of entry.

**Likelihood:** High | **Impact:** High

---

### Risk 3: Ransomware & Malware Infection
Given the college's reliance on digital systems for administration, teaching, and examination management, a ransomware attack would be catastrophic. Without adequate endpoint protection and offline backups, a successful attack could render all college systems inaccessible and result in the permanent loss of critical data including examination records and student files.

**Likelihood:** Medium-High | **Impact:** Critical

---

### Risk 4: Unauthorized Access Due to Weak Authentication
Several college systems currently rely on single-factor authentication (username and password only). Weak or reused passwords are easily compromised. Without Multi-Factor Authentication, a single stolen password gives an attacker full access to email, the LMS, or administrative systems. This is particularly concerning for accounts with elevated privileges such as staff administrators.

**Likelihood:** Medium | **Impact:** High

---

### Risk 5: Insider Threats & Excessive Access Privileges
Not all threats come from outside. Employees or students with excessive system access could intentionally or accidentally cause significant harm — from leaking exam papers to deleting records. Without proper access controls, audit logging, and a defined offboarding process, the college is exposed to threats from within.

**Likelihood:** Medium | **Impact:** High

---

## Top 5 Recommendations

### Recommendation 1: Implement a Formal Patch Management Programme
Establish a structured, documented process for applying security updates across all college systems. Critical patches should be applied within 48 hours of release. All systems should be inventoried and monitored for compliance. Eliminate or replace any end-of-life software that no longer receives manufacturer support.

**Expected Outcome:** Closes known vulnerabilities before attackers can exploit them. Significantly reduces the risk of ransomware and malware infections.

---

### Recommendation 2: Deploy Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) Across All Systems
Roll out MFA for all staff and student accounts — prioritizing email, the LMS, VPN access, and HR/payroll systems. Modern MFA solutions are low-cost and minimally disruptive to users while providing dramatic security improvements.

**Expected Outcome:** Prevents account takeovers even when passwords are compromised. Reduces the risk of unauthorized access by over 99% for accounts with MFA enabled.

---

### Recommendation 3: Launch an Ongoing Security Awareness Training Programme
Implement a regular training schedule covering phishing recognition, password security, safe data handling, and incident reporting. Include simulated phishing exercises to test and reinforce awareness in a practical, real-world way. All new staff and students should complete training during onboarding.

**Expected Outcome:** Significantly reduces successful phishing attacks and human-error-related incidents. Builds a security-conscious culture across the institution.

---

### Recommendation 4: Implement the 3-2-1 Backup Strategy
Ensure all critical data — student records, examination data, financial records — is backed up daily using the 3-2-1 strategy (3 copies, 2 different media, 1 offsite/offline). Test backup restoration procedures quarterly to confirm backups are reliable and complete.

**Expected Outcome:** In the event of a ransomware attack or hardware failure, the college can restore operations quickly without paying a ransom or losing critical data.

---

### Recommendation 5: Enforce Least Privilege Access Control & Strengthen Offboarding
Conduct an immediate audit of all user accounts and remove excessive access privileges. Implement Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) so users can only access systems relevant to their role. Create a formal offboarding process that immediately disables all access when an employee or contractor leaves.

**Expected Outcome:** Reduces the damage any single compromised or malicious account can cause. Eliminates the risk of former employees retaining active system access.

---

## Expected Benefits

Implementing the above recommendations will deliver the following benefits to the college:

| Benefit | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Reduced Risk of Data Breach** | Stronger access controls and MFA make unauthorized access significantly harder |
| **Ransomware Resilience** | Reliable backups and patched systems mean a ransomware attack is recoverable, not catastrophic |
| **Regulatory Compliance** | Demonstrates compliance with GDPR and UK data protection laws, avoiding fines |
| **Reputation Protection** | Preventing a high-profile breach protects the college's reputation with students, parents, and partners |
| **Business Continuity** | Robust defenses and a clear incident response plan ensure operations continue even during an incident |
| **Cost Savings** | The cost of implementing these measures is a fraction of the cost of recovering from a major cyber incident |

---

## Conclusion

The college faces a real and evolving cybersecurity threat landscape. However, the risks identified in this report are well understood and the recommended measures are proven, cost-effective, and achievable. Cybersecurity is not a one-time project — it is an ongoing commitment. By prioritizing these recommendations, the college will be well-positioned to protect its students, staff, and operations from the most significant threats it faces today.
